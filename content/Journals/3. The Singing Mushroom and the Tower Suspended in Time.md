Setting out from the Red Hammer, the party descended once more into the Cogs, following Tresca’s directions toward a cave system where a rare fire-resistant plant could be found. The path was dark and winding, and before long they came to a split in the passage. A biting unnatural chill emanated from the left tunnel, so cold it seemed to draw the warmth from their bones. Korruk, extending his senses, felt the unmistakable presence of something ancient, powerful, and malevolent, undead of terrifying potency. The group wisely chose to avoid that way.

The tunnels soon grew uneven, showing hints of an older civilization carved into the stone: ancient steps, fractured shelves, and half-buried archways. Eventually, they emerged into a vast chamber dominated by the ruins of a tower exploded apart in perfect stillness, with bricks, arches, fragments of stairways, and beams suspended in the air like the pieces of an engineering diagram frozen in an exploded view.

None could make sense of its strange nature. They knew Tresca had mentioned this place in her directions, but now the path forward seemed to elude them. Stake, ever confident, insisted the right-hand passage was the way forward. Saal tested the tower’s fragments, pressing one of the floating stones sideways; it shifted a few inches, then snapped back into place with impossible speed.

After a brief debate and some reminiscing between Korruk and Stake about a childhood hunting trip where Stake and their father got lost for two days (Stake maintaining even now that he knew the way and it was their father’s insistence that led them astray) the group decided to move on, following Stake’s lead.

As they departed, Saal noticed a faint glimmer on the floor: a purple crystal, roughly two inches wide and half a foot long. Stake immediately scooped it up. Strangely, it slipped through everyone’s grasp but his; even Saal could only touch it while Stake held it.

Korruk recalled, with a flash of natural 20-level clarity, an old tome describing such crystals being used by druids of the Shadow Marches as wards to seal away the Daelkyr. Deciding it was too curious to abandon, Stake pocketed it and they continued.

After nearly two hours of travel, the tunnels opened into a massive lava-lit cavern. The air shimmered with heat, veins of molten rock glowing along the walls and pooling across the ground. At its center rose a broad mound, twenty feet high in two gradual steps, crowned with moss and strange vegetation.

Eryx led the way, Stake close behind, while Korruk and Saal hung back. Scattered across the floor were several lizards the size of small dogs, basking in the warmth. When Eryx attempted to climb the mound, he slipped, sending a shower of stones tumbling. The noise drew attention, and a large lizard peered over the ledge. In moments, battle erupted.

Smaller lizards darted in from hidden crevices as the party fought to gain footing. Stake, charging forward on all fours, impaled one cleanly on his horn, a sight that gave even his companions pause. Saal, moving along a side passage, was blindsided by a much larger beast that knocked him flat.

Eryx, locked in combat with the lead lizard clinging to the mound, channeled divine might into his strike, cleaving the creature in two with radiant fury. Korruk, calling on his own dark blessings, unleashed necrotic power that withered another attacker, then rushed to heal Saal’s injuries.

Though revived, Saal was gravely wounded, his ribs badly bruised and blood still seeping through his tunic, leaving him weakened (–4 to max HP). He refused to move further until the wound was properly treated, and Eryx obliged with healing magic before they continued.

At the top of the mound, the party discovered a shallow pool of steaming water. Resting within it was something none of them expected: a red-and-orange-capped mushroom creature with a bright blue body, small nubby arms, and a wide, expressive mouth. It lounged lazily in the hot spring like a person soaking in a bath, eyeing the group warily.

Eryx tried speaking to it, but the creature only turned its head curiously, unable to communicate. Stake, ever impulsive, climbed into the pool beside it, earning a perturbed look from the mushroom, and began pouring warm water over its cap using the potato chip bowl he had stolen from the Red Hammer. The creature’s demeanor changed instantly: it smiled, then began to sing, a bubbling, melodic hum that echoed through the chamber. Each time Stake paused, the mushroom emitted a mournful grumble until he resumed.

After some moral debate about the ethics of what they were doing, the group agreed to proceed with care. When they attempted to remove the mushroom from its pool, it began to screech and flail, clearly distressed. Working together, they filled Stake’s bowl with the warm spring water and gently lifted the mushroom, who protested loudly until Saal used his magic to maintain the heat of the water and even recreated the sensation of pouring warm water over its head, complete with an illusion mimicking the creature’s own song. Though the mimicry was not perfect, it seemed to calm the being. Saal also filled his waterskin with the pool’s water, just in case.

Navigating back through the uneven tunnels was difficult, and Stake had to take great care not to spill the bowl or jostle their unusual passenger. When they reached the time-frozen tower chamber again, their sense of direction momentarily faltered, as if their thoughts were clouded, but only briefly. On a whim, Eryx pushed one of the floating stones directly toward the tower’s center. It moved a few inches before snapping back, but this time it emitted a pure, resonant tone, like a crystal bell.

Intrigued, the group experimented further. They found that each stone could only move a fixed distance, always producing the same tone, but that different stones moved by varying amounts and made distinctly different sounds. Deciding it was best not to linger, they pressed on toward the surface.

Further along, Stake slipped on the uneven ground, spilling most of the bowl’s water. The mushroom, clearly sentient in its own peculiar way, began to wail pitifully. Thinking fast, Saal refilled the bowl with his enchanted waterskin and reheated it with magic, soothing the creature back to contentment. The group wrapped the bowl carefully in a blanket and made their way back to Drummond’s forge.

They arrived to find Tresca, the gnome artificer, and Stoke, the warforged smith, hard at work finishing the Burning Idol. Tresca warned that while the idol was safe to carry, it contained the fires of Fernia, and dropping or striking it could unleash a burst of searing heat.

The pair were impressed (and slightly bemused) by the party’s success, especially their return with the “plant,” which turned out to be a sentient mushroom the group had nicknamed “the Sindrial.” The mushroom was now transfixed by the glowing Fernian firefly still hovering within the forge’s rune circle. Before handing it over, the party extracted a promise from Tresca that the Sindrial would not be harmed.

Tresca, protected by her enchanted snow globe, stepped into the circle. The Sindrial began to sing, and the dimming firefly responded, its lazy spiral carrying it downward toward the mushroom. At the last moment, a long tongue lashed out, snatching the firefly from the air and swallowing it whole.

With a delighted croak of “Hot! Spicy!”, the Sindrial began to cry pearlescent tears of red and blue. Tresca hurriedly collected them into a crystal vial. When she emerged from the forge circle, she announced that she had enough for six doses, two for the party, two for Drummond, and two for herself and Stoke.

She offered the group a choice: she could brew the tears into an enhanced Oil of Fire Resistance or a Potion of Fire Breath. The decision, as ever, was left to them.

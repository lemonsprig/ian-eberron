# Religion in Eberron

_In a world where gods stay silent, faith becomes a matter of choice, not proof._

## The Divine Silence

Eberron's gods, if they exist at all, do not speak to mortals. No divine avatars walk the land, no burning bushes deliver commandments, and no miraculous interventions settle theological debates. Yet divine magic flows freely to those with sufficient faith—suggesting that belief itself might be the source of power, regardless of whether its object truly exists.

This uncertainty shapes every aspect of Eberron's religious landscape. Faith becomes personal conviction rather than revealed truth, and religious authority stems from wisdom and charisma rather than divine mandate.

## The Sovereign Host: The Common Faith

_"Nine faces of divinity, watching over all aspects of life."_

**The Pantheon of Daily Life:** Most people across Khorvaire worship the Sovereign Host—nine deities who govern everything from war to agriculture, love to death. The Host offers something for everyone: farmers pray to Arawai for good harvests, soldiers honor Dol Dorn before battle, and lovers invoke Olladra for luck.

**The Nine Sovereigns:**

| Deity          | Domains                                     | Common Worshippers                                   |
| -------------- | ------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| **Arawai**     | Agriculture, fertility, plant life, weather | Farmers, gardeners, druids, expectant mothers        |
| **Aureon**     | Knowledge, law, lore, magic                 | Scholars, wizards, judges, scribes                   |
| **Balinor**    | Beasts, hunt, nature                        | Hunters, rangers, beast tamers, wilderness guides    |
| **Boldrei**    | Community, hearth, healing, peace           | Innkeepers, healers, community leaders, families     |
| **Dol Arrah**  | Honor, sacrifice, light, war                | Paladins, knights, those seeking redemption          |
| **Dol Dorn**   | Strength, steel, warfare                    | Soldiers, mercenaries, weapon smiths, athletes       |
| **Kol Korran** | Trade, travel, wealth                       | Merchants, traders, House Orien, bankers             |
| **Olladra**    | Feast, fortune, luck                        | Gamblers, entertainers, sailors, anyone needing luck |
| **Onatar**     | Artifice, fire, forge                       | Artificers, smiths, House Cannith, craftsmen         |

**Flexible Worship:** Host worship adapts to local needs. A farming community might emphasize Arawai and Balinor, while a trading port focuses on Kol Korran and Onatar. This adaptability helped the faith spread across the old Galifar Empire.

**The Dark Six:** The Host's shadow counterparts represent necessary but dangerous aspects of existence. Most people acknowledge them through placation rather than worship, though some embrace their darker gifts.

|Deity|Domains|Those Who Turn to Them|
|---|---|---|
|**The Devourer**|Destruction, nature's wrath, sea storms|Sailors facing storms, those seeking to destroy|
|**The Fury**|Madness, passion, revenge|The betrayed, those consumed by rage or grief|
|**The Keeper**|Death, decay, greed|Grave robbers, the desperate, those fearing death|
|**The Mockery**|Betrayal, dishonor, treachery|Spies, assassins, the dishonored seeking power|
|**The Shadow**|Dark magic, corruption, ambition|Warlocks, those seeking forbidden knowledge|
|**The Traveler**|Change, chaos, cunning, innovation|Changelings, revolutionaries, tricksters, inventors|

**Clerical Structure:** The Host has no central authority. Local priests interpret doctrine, perform ceremonies, and tend to their communities' spiritual needs. This creates regional variations but prevents religious wars over orthodoxy.

## The Silver Flame: The Crusading Light

_"The Flame burns away all corruption, leaving only purity behind."_

**The Voice of Righteousness:** The Silver Flame emerged as a distinct faith during Galifar's expansion, dedicated to fighting supernatural evil. Its followers believe a divine fire burns in Flamekeep's cathedral, offering guidance to those pure enough to hear its voice.

**The Theocracy of Thrane:** When Queen Diani died without an heir, the Church claimed divine mandate to rule Thrane. Today, eleven-year-old Jaela Daran serves as both Keeper of the Flame and the nation's temporal ruler—a situation that makes neighboring kingdoms nervous.

**The Templar Orders:** Elite warriors serve the Flame's cause, hunting undead, demons, lycanthropes, and other supernatural threats. Their dedication is absolute, their methods sometimes extreme, and their authority largely unquestioned within Thrane.

**The Purified:** The church preaches that the Flame burns away sin and corruption, creating a hierarchy based on spiritual purity. This doctrine attracts the genuinely devout but also enables zealotry and persecution.

**Beyond Thrane:** Silver Flame missionaries operate throughout Khorvaire, often in tension with local authorities and Host worship. Their anti-undead stance makes them especially unwelcome in Karrnath.

## The Blood of Vol: Death and Undeath

_"Death is the great enemy, and undeath is the weapon we wield against it."_

**The Forbidden Faith:** Officially, the Blood of Vol is a heretical cult that worships undeath and necromancy. In practice, it's a complex philosophy that sees death as the ultimate evil and undeath as a necessary tool for fighting it.

**The Lich Queen:** Vol's followers believe their undead matriarch will eventually defeat death itself, freeing mortals from their greatest fear. This belief system attracts those who've lost everything to war, plague, or violence.

**Karrnathi Connections:** During the Last War's darkest days, King Kaius II embraced Vol's teachings to bolster his armies with undead soldiers. Though his son officially renounced this alliance, many Karrnathi still practice Vol's rites in secret.

**The Emerald Claw:** Vol's militant arm seeks to hasten their goddess's victory through violence and terror. These extremists give the entire faith a reputation for evil that more moderate practitioners struggle to overcome.

## The Cults of the Dragon Below

_"In the depths below, ancient powers stir and dream of dominion."_

**Madness from the Depths:** These loosely connected cults worship the daelkyr—alien entities imprisoned beneath Eberron's surface. Their influence seeps upward, corrupting minds and flesh in equal measure.

**The Aberrant Dragonmarks:** Some who bear twisted, aberrant dragonmarks believe these mutations are gifts from the Dragon Below. They seek to unlock their marks' full potential, often with catastrophic results.

**Regional Variations:** In the Shadow Marches, orc cults remember when the daelkyr walked the surface. Sharn's depths hide cells of human cultists seeking forbidden knowledge. The Mournland's corruption attracts those who see beauty in twisted transformation.

**The Gatekeepers' Watch:** Druid circles descended from those who first imprisoned the daelkyr maintain constant vigilance against the cults' activities, viewing them as existential threats to all life.

## Faith in Practice

**Personal Choice:** Without divine revelation to settle disputes, religious belief becomes deeply personal. Communities might share temples, individuals might honor multiple faiths, and conversion happens through conviction rather than coercion.

**Magical Reality:** Divine magic works regardless of theological correctness, suggesting that faith itself—not its specific object—generates power. This raises uncomfortable questions about the nature of divinity that most people prefer not to examine too closely.

**War's Impact:** The Last War strengthened some faiths while weakening others. The Silver Flame's crusading spirit attracted those seeking purpose, while the Sovereign Host's flexibility helped communities endure hardship. The Blood of Vol gained followers among those who'd lost everything.

**Social Functions:** Religious institutions provide education, charity, and community organization. They marry couples, bless crops, counsel the troubled, and tend the sick—functions that matter more to most people than theological fine points.

## Regional Differences

**Aundair:** Sophisticated theological debates flourish alongside practical magic research. Many scholars study religion as philosophy rather than revelation.

**Breland:** Religious diversity thrives under democratic ideals. Immigrants bring their own faiths, creating a vibrant spiritual marketplace.

**Karrnath:** The Blood of Vol's influence lingers despite official renunciation. Many Karrnathi practice a pragmatic form of Host worship that acknowledges death's reality.

**Thrane:** Silver Flame orthodoxy dominates public life, though private belief often proves more nuanced than official doctrine suggests.

**The Mournland:** Religious certainties collapsed with Cyre itself. Survivors question why their gods remained silent during the Mourning, leading to crisis of faith or desperate searches for new meaning.

## For Player Characters

**Divine Magic Without Certainty:** Clerics and paladins must decide what they believe and why, without divine confirmation of their choices.

**Cultural Context:** Your character's homeland shapes their religious background, but personal belief remains a choice.

**Social Expectations:** Religious affiliation affects how NPCs react to you. A Blood of Vol priest faces suspicion, while a Silver Flame templar commands respect—and fear.

**Adventure Hooks:** Religious conflicts drive many adventures. Hunt cultists, protect pilgrims, investigate heretical teachings, or explore the nature of faith itself.

**Moral Ambiguity:** Without divine commandments, ethical choices become personal decisions. What's righteous to one faith might be heretical to another.
